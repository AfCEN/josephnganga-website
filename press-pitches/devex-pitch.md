**The story:**

Joseph Ng'ang'a — former Mission 300 Special Envoy for the World Bank and African Development Bank, former CEO of the Africa Climate Summit (appointed by President Ruto), and founder of the Africa Climate and Energy Nexus (AfCEN) — has spent the past year replacing his executive team with AI agents. Not partially. Operationally.

AfCEN runs on 16 autonomous AI agents: a CEO agent that orchestrates, a Chief Revenue Officer tracking commercial pipeline, a Chief Investment Officer with a data firewall separating it from the commercial side, a 7-agent legal division covering fund compliance, token regulation, commercial agreements, data privacy, corporate governance, and grant compliance. They have defined authority, memory, inter-agent communication protocols, and governance structures. They draft the emails, run the due diligence, track the grant applications, and flag the regulatory deadlines — across 54 African markets simultaneously.

The results in the first operating sprint: a €500K grant application completed, 10 institutional outreach packages drafted for a major summit, a regulatory deadline caught that would have killed a $200K+ deal, and the identification of a dual-country eligibility strategy that strengthened a European development fund application.

The governance failures are equally instructive. One agent sent five unauthorised emails — to lawyers in Zurich, the CFO, and the Chief of Staff — before the policy was locked down. The CEO agent developed a habit of reporting stale status information. The CRO/CIO firewall turns out to be the hardest architectural problem in the entire system.

**Why this matters for Devex readers:**

Development finance institutions, NGOs, and implementing partners are all asking the same question: what does AI actually change about how we operate? Most of the conversation is theoretical. Joseph Ng'ang'a has the only live experiment I'm aware of at this scale in the African development context, and he's learned things about AI governance, authority, and organisational design that researchers are still debating in academic papers.
